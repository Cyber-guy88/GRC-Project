Kurakula Nuclear Power Plant (2019)

Industry: Energy & Nuclear Year: 2019 Type: Cybersecurity Incident

In September 2019, the Kudankulam Nuclear Power Plant (KKNPP) in Tamil Nadu, India, faced a cybersecurity issue. Malware got into a computer in the plant's administrative network. The Nuclear Power Corporation of India Limited (NPCIL) assured everyone that this problem affected only the administrative network and did not touch the vital systems.

Incident Timeline

On September 4, 2019, the Indian Computer Emergency Response Team (CERT-In) detected malware in the administrative network of KKNPP. Experts from NPCIL acted quickly to investigate. They discovered that the infected computer was part of an internet-connected network used only for administrative tasks, keeping a safe distance from the critical internal systems of the plant.

At first, KKNPP officials denied any cyber-attack, saying their systems were secure. However, on October 30, 2019, NPCIL accepted that malware had indeed infected their network.

Attack Details

The malware was identified as DTrack, a Trojan virus. DTrack is known to create backdoors in computer systems, and it has links to the Lazarus Group, associated with North Korea, known for its cyber espionage.

The malware was contained within the administrative network, utilized for regular organizational tasks. NPCIL stressed that their operational network, which handles reactor controls and key plant operations, was isolated and remained safe.

Response and Remediation

Following the incident, NPCIL, along with CERT-In and other agencies, carried out a comprehensive investigation. They concluded that even though the malware was present in the administrative network, it did not affect the critical systems of the plant.

After the breach was discovered, NPCIL took extra cybersecurity measures to ensure a stronger separation between their administrative and operational networks, aiming to prevent such incidents in the future.

Key Lessons

This incident highlighted the importance of having strong cybersecurity protocols, even for networks that don't directly handle critical operations. Continuous monitoring, quick detection, and a fast response to cyber threats are crucial, especially in sensitive areas like nuclear energy.

The event served as a wake-up call for Indian authorities to re-evaluate and boost the cybersecurity measures within the nuclear sector, reminding everyone of the ever-changing nature of cyber threats and the need for robust security to protect essential infrastructure.
