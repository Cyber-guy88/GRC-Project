Overview

The California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) is a state statute intended to enhance privacy rights and consumer protection for residents of California. Signed into law on June 28, 2018, and effective as of January 1, 2020, the CCPA establishes a framework for consumers to understand and control the personal information that businesses collect about them.

The CCPA was the first comprehensive consumer privacy law passed in the United States, creating a precedent for other states to follow in establishing similar privacy frameworks.

Consumer Rights

The CCPA grants California residents specific rights regarding their personal information. These rights empower consumers to understand and control how businesses use their data.

Right to Know

Consumers can request that businesses disclose what personal information they collect, use, share, or sell.

Right to Delete

Consumers can request that a business delete their personal information.

Right to Opt-Out

Consumers can opt-out of the sale of their personal information to third parties.

Right to Non-Discrimination

Businesses cannot discriminate against consumers who exercise their privacy rights.

Right to Opt-In for Minors

Children under 16 must opt-in for the sale of their personal information (with parental consent for under 13).

Right to Data Portability

Consumers can receive their personal information in a readily usable format that can be transferred to another entity.

Scope and Applicability

The CCPA applies to for-profit businesses that collect personal information from California residents and meet certain thresholds. Understanding the scope of the CCPA is critical for determining if a business must comply.

Business Criteria

The CCPA applies to businesses that meet at least one of the following criteria:

  • Annual gross revenues exceed $25 million
  • Buys, sells, or shares personal information of 50,000 or more consumers, households, or devices annually
  • Derives 50% or more of annual revenue from selling consumers' personal information

Covered Entities

The law applies to various entities:

  • For-profit businesses meeting the thresholds
  • Entities that control or are controlled by a business and share common branding
  • Joint ventures or partnerships where the business has at least a 40% interest

Personal Information

The CCPA broadly defines personal information as information that identifies, relates to, describes, or could reasonably be linked to a consumer or household, including:

  • Identifiers (name, address, email, SSN, etc.)
  • Commercial information (purchase records)
  • Biometric data
  • Internet activity (browsing history, search history)
  • Geolocation data
  • Professional or employment information
  • Education information
  • Inferences drawn from any of the above

Exemptions

Certain types of information are exempt from CCPA requirements:

  • Deidentified or aggregate consumer information
  • Medical information governed by HIPAA
  • Information collected as part of clinical trials
  • Information covered by the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA)
  • Personal information covered by the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (GLBA)
  • Information covered by the Driver's Privacy Protection Act

Business Obligations

Businesses subject to the CCPA must comply with various requirements to protect consumer privacy and enable consumers to exercise their rights. These obligations form the foundation of CCPA compliance.

Privacy Policy

Maintain a comprehensive privacy policy that discloses:

  • Categories of personal information collected
  • Purposes for collection
  • Categories of third parties with whom information is shared
  • Consumer rights under the CCPA
  • How to exercise those rights

Notice at Collection

Provide notice at or before collecting personal information:

  • Categories of information collected
  • Purposes for each category
  • Whether information is sold or shared
  • Retention period for each category

Opt-Out Mechanism

Implement mechanisms for consumers to opt-out:

  • "Do Not Sell or Share My Personal Information" link
  • Honor "Do Not Track" signals (optional)
  • Respect Global Privacy Control signals
  • Provide at least two methods for submitting requests

Response Timelines

Adhere to specific timelines for handling consumer requests:

  • Confirm receipt within 10 business days
  • Respond to requests within 45 calendar days
  • Possible 45-day extension with notice
  • Verify identity of requesters

Data Security

Implement reasonable security measures:

  • Implement and maintain reasonable security procedures
  • Protect personal information from unauthorized access
  • Ensure service providers provide similar protection
  • May face private lawsuits for data breaches

Verification Procedures

Establish procedures to verify consumer requests:

  • Develop verification methods based on sensitivity
  • Avoid requesting additional information when possible
  • Document verification process
  • Maintain records of requests and responses

Enforcement and Penalties

The CCPA is primarily enforced by the California Attorney General, with the California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA) gaining enforcement authority under the CPRA starting July 1, 2023. Understanding the enforcement mechanisms and potential penalties is essential for businesses subject to the law.

Regulatory Enforcement

The California Attorney General and CPPA can enforce the CCPA through investigations, administrative proceedings, and civil actions. Prior to initiating enforcement action, businesses typically receive a 30-day notice to cure violations (this cure period expired under CPRA on January 1, 2023, except in limited cases).

Unintentional Violations
Up to $2,500 per violation
Intentional Violations
Up to $7,500 per violation
Violations Involving Minors
Up to $7,500 per violation (even if unintentional)

Private Right of Action

Consumers have a limited private right of action for data breaches resulting from a business's failure to implement reasonable security procedures. This applies only to data breaches, not to other violations of the CCPA.

Statutory Damages
$100-$750 per consumer per incident, or actual damages, whichever is greater

Before filing a lawsuit, consumers must provide the business with 30 days' written notice identifying the specific violations. If the business cures the violation and provides an express written statement that the violations have been cured and no further violations will occur, the consumer cannot proceed with the action.

Audits and Investigations

The CPPA has the authority to conduct audits and investigations, including regular audits to ensure businesses' compliance with the law. These audits can be initiated at the agency's discretion or in response to consumer complaints.

Why It Matters

The CCPA represents a significant shift in how businesses approach data privacy in the United States. Its implementation has far-reaching implications for both businesses and consumers.

Sets a National Precedent

As the first comprehensive consumer privacy law in the U.S., the CCPA has influenced other states to adopt similar legislation, potentially leading to a national standard.

Empowers Consumers

By granting specific rights over personal information, the CCPA shifts the balance of power and gives consumers greater control over their data.

Enhances Data Protection

The law incentivizes businesses to implement stronger data security measures and more transparent data practices.

Drives Awareness

CCPA has increased general awareness about data privacy issues among businesses and consumers alike.

Improves Business Practices

Compliance requires businesses to gain better understanding of their data practices, often leading to operational improvements.

Global Impact

Many global businesses have extended CCPA-like protections to all users, rather than creating California-specific processes.

Implementation Challenges

While the CCPA has established important privacy protections, businesses face several challenges in implementing and maintaining compliance with the law.

Data Mapping Complexity

Many organizations struggle to identify and map all personal information in their systems, especially in complex or legacy environments.

Evolving Requirements

Ongoing amendments and regulatory changes require businesses to continuously update their compliance programs.

Vendor Management

Ensuring service providers and contractors handle data according to CCPA requirements adds complexity to vendor relationships.

Implementation Costs

Significant resources may be required for technology systems, process changes, and ongoing compliance operations.

Technical Infrastructure

Legacy systems may not support the granular data access, deletion, and portability requirements of the CCPA.

Identity Verification

Balancing the need to verify consumer identity against the prohibition on collecting additional personal information.

Despite these challenges, the CCPA represents an important step forward in U.S. privacy law. As businesses adapt to these requirements and as additional states implement similar laws, a more comprehensive approach to privacy protection continues to evolve in the United States.
